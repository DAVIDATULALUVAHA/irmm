Intial Post

The authors used the Qualitative methods because no knowledge had previously been published in the domain of Security Risk Management(SRM) in relation to user involvement. Qualitative methods provided a better comprehension of user mannerisms and tasks in tandem with user involvement in SRM. Qualitative methods helped create a process model that produced definite results. They used Quantitative methods to measure the theoretical model resulting from the qualitative study. Both qualitative and quantitative methods gave a better context and measurability to their study hence better outcomes. (Spears & Barki, 2010)

Under the buy-in theory of participation, user involvement:

  1. Raises organizational awareness of Information security risks and controls.
  2. Contributes to an alignment between SRM and the business context.
  3. Leads to more organizational awareness of IS security. (Spears & Barki, 2010)

Peer response Post by Doug Leece

Hi David, I concur that those closest to the impacts should be consulted in a risk assessment and when performing risk assessment work within an organization it is important to gather as many points of view as possible. Not only are such measures important for reducing bias, the can also identifying interdependencies that may not be apparent to individuals surveyed.

An entire chapter of the first book on Factor Analysis Information Risk (FAIR) is focused on how to reliably interpret expert estimates on threats and impacts (Freund & Jones, 2014) indicating this is a problem other risk professionals have experienced. This project offers no options for interactions with ACME users so identification of risk may need to be somewhat of a thought or research exercise with well stated assumptions.

References

  1. Freund, J. & Jones, J. (2014) Measuring and Managing Information Risk. 1st ed. Waltham: Butterworth-Heinemann.
